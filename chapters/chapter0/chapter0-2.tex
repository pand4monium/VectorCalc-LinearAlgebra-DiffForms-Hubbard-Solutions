\section{Quantifiers and Negation}

\bx{ % 0.2.1
    Negate the following statements: \ea{
        \item Every prime number such that if you divide it by 4 you have a remainder of 1 is the sum of two squares.
        \item For all $x \in \mathbb R$ and for all $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $y \in \mathbb R$, if $|y -x| < \delta$, then $|y^2 - x^2| < \epsilon$.
        \item For all $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in \mathbb R$, if $|y - x| < \delta$, then $|y^2 - x^2| < \epsilon$.
    }
    \sol{
        \ea{
        \item There exists a prime number in the form of $4n + 1$, for $n \in \mathbb N_0$ that cannot be expressed as the sum of two squares.
        \item There exists $x \in \mathbb R$ and $\epsilon > 0$ such that for all $\delta > 0$, there exists $ y \in \mathbb R$ with $|y-x| < \delta$ but $|y^2 - x^2| \ge \epsilon$.
        \item There exists $\epsilon > 0$ such that for all $\delta > 0$, there exists $x, y \in \mathbb R$ with $|y-x| < \delta$ but $|y^2 - x^2| \ge \epsilon$.
    }}
}

\bx{ % 0.2.2
    Explain why one of these statements is true and the other is false: \begin{align*}
        (\forall \text{ man } M)(\exists \text{ woman } W) \; &| \; W \text{ is the mother of M} \\ 
        (\exists \text{ woman } W)(\forall \text{ man } M) \; &| \; W \text{ is the mother of M}
    \end{align*}
    \sol{
        The difference between the two statements lies in the quantifier. Obviously the first statement is true while the second is false.


        The first statement states that for every man, we can find a woman that is the man's father. This is inherently true as every man must have a mother.

        On the other hand, the second statement states that we can find a women that is the mother of every man, which is obviously not possible.
    }
}
